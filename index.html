Wisp - Roadtrips revisited.

Wisp was a project through my Service Design course that was an examination of the current state of long distance travel on car.


First, we needed to figure out the current states of the roadtrips as is and establish our stakeholders to design towards using affinity diagrams.


My group and I explored this market using qualitative data gained from five different research methods: interviews, competitive analyses, storyboarding, speed dating, and user enactments.


With the result of all of our research and findings, we took a step back to our initial customer journey and stakeholder models to produce our value flow diagram and revised customer journey.


Using the results of our research, my team and I designed a mockup and wireframe of a possible interface for Wisp in your car, as well as a video describing the benefits and use cases of our concept.

  • Class: Service Design
  • Date: Fall 2015

CarnegieHERE


Carnegie HERE is an application created by a small team of students enrolled in the Ideate Fall 2015 course Mobile Application Development & Design. My specific role was in our UI/UX team as well as the frontend development and content teams as well. And a special thanks to YinzCam for funding and helping the course!


First, my team and I had to agree on the scope of features within our app. This meant we had to know what we could reasonably complete with just a semester with maybe a few stretch goals. Once these limitations were established, we were able to map out the paths users could pick to access different functions.


Parallel to this process, I also had to develop designs for the layout, icons, and overall aesthetic of the app. Then, the designs could be shipped off to the frontend development team that was working alongside of us.


By the end of the semester, we created a robust app that could be exported to iOS and Android that featured: iBeacon hardware functionality, personal and interest-based filters, and location-aware notifications for classes, assignments, events, and messages. We presented our progress to the Pittsburgh Penguins and Pittsburgh Children Hospital who offered us their suggestions and thoughts on the future of IoT.

  • Class: Mobile Application Design & Development
  • Date: Fall 2015

Health Literacy - Family Meal Planner

\


I worked on this project with two other group members in unison with the Urban League of Greater Pittsburgh and the Regional Health Literacy Coalition in order to design a usable solution for members of the Duquesne community. The people living here often find themselves in unbalanced diets lacking nutritional value.
While the root cause of the problem leads back to economic challenges and unhealthy environments, my team and I worked to bring light to the healthy opportunities that are still available for them to take. Through surveys, on-site interviews and meetings, and consulting with our experienced representatives in the Urban League and RHLC, we were able to design a book and pocket-card to address the issue of finding nutritious food in unhealthy environments with limited transportation and funds.

  • Class: Designing for Health Literacy & Information
  • Date: Fall 2015

Front Desk


For this project, my group and I focused on innovation within the hotel business. Right now, there is a prominent leader in the mobile space for all service based industries. However, the hotel business lacks an industry leader and there is a definitive space to introduce a new user experience for customers and hotel staff alike.

We began defining the goals and use cases of the application by creating potential personas and user scenarios.


After talking to nearby hotels in the area, ranging from local ones like Shadyside Inn to big chains like the Wyndham, we received valuable feedback beyond the consumer side into the "backend" of the hotel staff and management. Using this new perspective, we also designed a management-side interface to view analytics, schedules, and maintain requests incoming from consumers. (Powerpoint is a prototype of an actual interface.)

Rings for a Nerve


My project proposal was chosen out of 50 in our class where I wanted to target the inaccessibility of affordable prosthetics for people suffering from nerve damage, arthritis, or Parkinson's Disease to use everyday objects like paint brushes, pencils, and cutlery. Initially, I had a prototype that fit my requirements for being: easily replicable, lightweight, and versatile.


Eventually, we decided to approach this problem by offering a more robust method of simple, easy scans of people's grips on clay molds. Then, once we scan these molds with 123D Catch on any smartphone, we can get a 3D model .STL file that requires only a little editing to make viable for our Makerbot Simple printers. Finally, a hole is drilled in any range of sizes for any range of tools you might need this prostetic for.

  • Class: Applied Fabrication Techniques
  • Date: Fall 2014
